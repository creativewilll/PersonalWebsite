# The Stack Overflow + OpenAI Partnership: Why Developers Revolted

**Stack Overflow just announced a partnership with OpenAI that has the developer community in open revolt.** Three days after the news broke, moderators are protesting, high-reputation users are deleting their content, and the platform that once billed itself as "by developers, for developers" is facing an existential trust crisis. This isn't just another AI deal—it's a flashpoint in the broader war over who owns the knowledge we've collectively built, and whether the platforms that host it have the right to monetize our contributions without meaningful consent.

---

## What Just Happened: The Partnership Announcement

**On May 6, 2024, Stack Overflow and OpenAI announced a strategic API partnership that integrates 15 years of community-contributed programming knowledge directly into ChatGPT.** The deal gives OpenAI access to Stack Overflow's OverflowAPI product, which contains over 59 million questions and answers accumulated since the platform's launch in 2008. In exchange, Stack Overflow gains access to OpenAI's models to accelerate development of OverflowAI, their own generative AI capabilities.

The timing couldn't be more charged. Just 18 months earlier, Stack Overflow made headlines by banning ChatGPT-generated answers from the platform entirely, citing quality concerns and the risk of "AI slop" flooding the community. Now the same platform is formally partnering with the company behind that banned tool—creating an immediate contradiction that developers are calling a betrayal of trust.

Within hours of the announcement, the reaction across developer forums, Reddit, and Hacker News is uniformly hostile. High-reputation users who have spent years contributing thousands of answers are publicly declaring they will delete their content. Moderators are organizing protests. The platform that positioned itself as "by developers, for developers" is facing an existential legitimacy crisis—and this is only day one.

---

## The Deal Structure: What Stack Overflow and OpenAI Actually Agreed To

**The partnership is structured as a reciprocal data-and-API exchange with undisclosed financial terms.** OpenAI receives continuous access to Stack Overflow's public dataset through the OverflowAPI product, giving their models a direct pipeline to vetted technical knowledge, code samples, and community feedback. Stack Overflow receives access to OpenAI's models to power OverflowAI, their planned generative AI features for both public and enterprise users.

| Component | What OpenAI Gets | What Stack Overflow Gets |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Data Access** | OverflowAPI with 59M+ Q&A pairs, 15 years of code and technical discussions | Model access for AI feature development |
| **Attribution** | Commitment to surface Stack Overflow content with links back to source posts | "Insights" from OpenAI's internal model testing |
| **Timeline** | First integrations expected by end of June 2024 | AI Assist features launching alongside |
| **Financial Terms** | Undisclosed licensing fees | Undisclosed revenue share |

**The attribution mechanism remains vague.** OpenAI promises that ChatGPT will "surface validated technical knowledge from Stack Overflow with attribution and links back to relevant posts," but the actual implementation details—how prominently links appear, whether contributors are named individually, or if this applies only to verbatim quotes versus training-derived knowledge—are unspecified. Developers who have watched AI models absorb and regurgitate their code without credit are skeptical that attribution will be meaningful.

**The financial opacity is equally problematic.** Stack Overflow CEO Prashanth Chandrasekar has acknowledged that data licensing is now a core business model for the company, calling it essential to "adopt a new business model to survive." Yet there is zero indication that the community members whose contributions generate this revenue will receive any compensation. The deal represents a classic platform playbook: build network effects on volunteer labor, then monetize without revenue sharing.

## Why Developers Are Furious: Four Core Grievances

**The backlash isn't merely about a single partnership—it's a fundamental breakdown of the social contract between Stack Overflow and its contributing community.** Developers who have spent years answering questions, curating content, and building the platform's knowledge base feel that Stack Overflow Inc. is treating their volunteer labor as a proprietary asset to be sold without consent or compensation. The grievances cluster around four interconnected issues.

### The Broken Social Contract

**Stack Overflow was built on an explicit promise: "by developers, for developers."** The platform's original mission emphasized community ownership of knowledge, with contributors retaining rights to their content under Creative Commons licenses. The implicit bargain was that contributions would remain a public good, accessible to all developers solving similar problems—not that they would become training fuel for proprietary AI models owned by third-party corporations.

The partnership represents a mission drift that many contributors find intellectually dishonest. Stack Overflow positioned itself as a neutral community resource, not a data broker. The pivot to "enterprise SaaS business with AI-based solutions and a data licensing business"—as Chandrasekar now describes it—was never presented to the community as the endgame when they were contributing their expertise.

### Attribution and the Erosion of Credit

**AI training fundamentally breaks the attribution chain that Creative Commons licenses require.** When a model trains on a Stack Overflow answer, that knowledge becomes embedded in neural network weights—not referenced or linked. The model may output code or explanations derived from a specific contributor's work without any indication of the source, let alone credit to the original author.

OpenAI's promised attribution only applies to direct quotations surfaced in ChatGPT responses. But the vast majority of training value comes from the model internalizing patterns, approaches, and solutions—not from verbatim regurgitation. A developer whose elegant algorithm or debugging technique gets incorporated into GPT-5's training data will never know, and will never be credited, even if that technique becomes part of millions of AI-generated responses.

### Uncompensated Value Extraction

**The economics are stark: volunteer contributors build the asset; Stack Overflow Inc. captures the value.** Contributors donated their time and expertise with the understanding that they were helping a community resource. Now that same expertise is being sold in bulk to the highest bidder—OpenAI today, presumably Google Anthropic and others tomorrow—with zero revenue sharing back to the individuals who created the value.

Chandrasekar has hinted that "in the future, we may consider other ways" to share revenue with users, including paying contributors a piece of data licensing revenue. But this is vague speculation, not policy. The deals are being signed now. The checks are being cashed now. The community sees a platform taking their work product to market while offering only future possibilities in return.

### Building Tools That Replace the Builders

**The ultimate irony: community knowledge is being used to train models that make the community itself unnecessary.** Stack Overflow's value proposition has always been that human experts provide better answers than documentation alone. Now that same human-curated knowledge is training AI systems that answer programming questions directly—systems that may eventually reduce the need for developers to visit Stack Overflow at all.

The 2022 ban on ChatGPT-generated answers recognized this threat explicitly: AI-generated responses risked flooding the platform with plausible-sounding but potentially incorrect information, undermining trust. Now Stack Overflow is partnering with the source of that threat, effectively acknowledging that the future of technical knowledge consumption is AI-mediated—and positioning themselves as the data supplier rather than the destination.

## The Moderator Revolt: When Community Leaders Rebel

**The backlash has moved from forum complaints to active resistance, with moderators and high-reputation users organizing coordinated protests.** Within 24 hours of the announcement, posts appear on Meta Stack Overflow discussing content deletion, with some users explicitly stating they are removing or defacing their answers to prevent them from being used as AI training data.

Stack Overflow's response has been aggressive: the platform is banning users en masse for deleting their own content as a form of protest against the OpenAI partnership. Users who attempt to remove contributions they authored are finding their accounts suspended, with moderation actions treating content deletion as vandalism rather than a legitimate response to changed platform terms.

The dynamic is particularly fraught because Stack Overflow's moderator corps—volunteers who handle flagging, editing, and community management—are simultaneously the platform's most invested contributors and the ones most likely to understand the technical and ethical implications of the AI deal. These are users with tens of thousands of reputation points who have shaped the platform's culture for years. Their rebellion isn't casual dissent; it's a vote of no confidence from the community's leadership tier.

**The strike threat is real.** Moderators are discussing work stoppages, content lockouts, and mass resignation from moderation duties. The implication is clear: if Stack Overflow Inc. won't respect the community that built the platform, the community may stop maintaining it.

## Historical Context: This Isn't Stack Overflow's First Trust Crisis

**Stack Overflow has a documented pattern of platform-community conflicts that have eroded trust over years.** The current AI partnership revolt is the latest in a series of crises that reveal a fundamental tension between Stack Overflow Inc.'s commercial imperatives and its community-driven origins.

The **Monica Cellio incident** (2019) stands as the most significant prior breach. A longtime moderator and respected community member was summarily dismissed without clear process, triggering a wave of moderator resignations and establishing a reputation for Stack Overflow Inc. prioritizing corporate control over community relationships. The incident demonstrated that even the most dedicated contributors were expendable when they conflicted with company direction.

The **2018 license confusion** created additional legal ambiguity. Stack Overflow briefly changed the licensing terms on user content from CC BY-SA 3.0 to 4.0 without clear community consultation, then walked back the change amid outcry. The episode left lingering questions about who actually controls the licensing of contributed content—and whether Stack Overflow Inc. can unilaterally relicense community work for commercial purposes.

More recently, the **June 2023 moderator strike** over AI content moderation policies showed how contentious the AI issue already was. Moderators protested company-mandated changes to how AI-generated content would be handled, feeling that Stack Overflow Inc. was overriding community standards. The strike ended with a compromise, but the underlying tension—corporate AI strategy versus community values—never resolved.

Each crisis has followed a similar pattern: company makes unilateral decision, community reacts with betrayal and anger, company offers partial concessions or communications outreach, trust is partially restored but never fully. The cumulative effect is a community that now defaults to suspicion when Stack Overflow Inc. announces major changes—especially changes involving monetization of their contributions.

## The Broader Pattern: Platforms vs. Communities in the AI Era

**Stack Overflow is not alone in facing community revolt over AI partnerships.** Across the internet, platforms built on user-generated content are discovering that the same community contributions that built their value are now their most monetizable asset—and that the communities themselves don't appreciate being treated as raw material.

### Reddit's Parallel Play

**Reddit announced its own OpenAI partnership just weeks before Stack Overflow, with strikingly similar terms.** The deal gives OpenAI access to Reddit's data API to train models on user posts and conversations, while Reddit gets AI-powered features for its platform. The announcement triggered comparable backlash, with Redditors expressing anger that their conversations would be used to train AI without consent.

Reddit's AI deals follow closely on the **API pricing crisis of 2023**, when the company dramatically increased API access costs, effectively killing third-party Reddit apps and triggering a site-wide protest that saw thousands of communities go dark. The pattern is consistent: Reddit treats its community's content and engagement as proprietary assets to be sold to the highest bidder, community sentiment be damned.

### The Platform Playbook

**A clear template is emerging for how platforms navigate the AI transition:**

1. **Phase 1: Build network effects** — Create a platform that attracts user-generated content through network effects, positioning it as a community resource or public good.

2. **Phase 2: Restrict access** — Gradually limit API access, data exports, and third-party use, consolidating control over the accumulated content.

3. **Phase 3: Monetize with AI companies** — Strike exclusive or semi-exclusive deals with AI labs to sell training access to the accumulated data, often with attribution promises that satisfy legal requirements without meaningfully crediting individual contributors.

4. **Phase 4: Deflect backlash** — Issue statements about "partnership benefits for users," promise vague future revenue sharing, and enforce platform terms that prevent users from removing their contributions to protest.

The platforms following this playbook—Reddit, Stack Overflow, potentially Quora and others—share a common characteristic: they are **content aggregators that became data landlords**. The communities that created the value are discovering they are tenants, not owners, and the rent is being collected by platform operators with no obligation to share proceeds.

## The Legal and Ethical Gray Zones

**The Stack Overflow OpenAI partnership sits at the intersection of unsettled legal questions about AI training data, user content rights, and platform terms of service.** Whether the deal is legally defensible remains unclear; whether it is ethically justifiable is even murkier.

### What the ToS Actually Says

**Stack Overflow's Terms of Service do grant broad rights to the platform regarding user content.** By posting, users grant Stack Overflow "a perpetual and irrevocable license and right to use, copy, cache, publish, display, distribute, modify, create derivative works, store, and archive" their contributions. This language, standard for user-generated content platforms, theoretically covers the AI training use case.

However, the ToS also specifies that content is licensed under Creative Commons terms—primarily CC BY-SA 3.0 for contributions before May 2018, and CC BY-SA 4.0 thereafter. The interplay between these two frameworks—broad platform rights versus specific copyleft licensing terms—has never been tested in court for AI training scenarios.

**The critical question:** Does a platform's broad ToS grant override the specific attribution and share-alike requirements of Creative Commons licenses when the use case (AI model training) was not contemplated when users agreed to the terms?

### The Creative Commons Complication

**Creative Commons licenses require attribution and, for share-alike variants, that derivative works be shared under the same license.** Stack Overflow uses CC BY-SA (Attribution-ShareAlike), which theoretically requires that any derivative works—including, arguably, AI models trained on the content—be made available under the same open license.

AI companies have generally taken the position that training on data is fair use, not creation of a derivative work. This interpretation has not been definitively tested in court for generative AI models, though lawsuits are pending. If courts ultimately rule that model training does create a derivative work, the implications for CC-licensed training data are profound: OpenAI might be required to release model weights under open licenses, or Stack Overflow might be in breach of the licenses under which its community contributed content.

### The Consent Gap

**The most damning critique is also the simplest: users never consented to this specific use case.** When developers contributed answers to Stack Overflow in 2012, 2015, or 2020, they understood they were helping other developers solve problems. They did not agree—explicitly or implicitly—to have their contributions sold as training data for proprietary AI systems.

The ethical gap is between what is technically permitted by ToS legalese and what users reasonably understood they were agreeing to. Stack Overflow's defense—that the ToS covers this use—ignores the question of whether users would have contributed if they understood the ultimate destination of their work. The consent obtained was for a community Q&A platform, not a data licensing business supplying AI model training sets.

## What This Means for the Future of Knowledge Sharing

**The Stack Overflow OpenAI partnership signals a fundamental shift in how technical knowledge will be created, shared, and monetized going forward.** Whether this represents the end of an era for community-driven knowledge platforms or merely a painful transition depends on how developers respond—and what alternatives emerge.

### The Contribution Chilling Effect

**Developers who would have contributed answers to Stack Overflow may now think twice.** The knowledge that contributions become training fuel for AI models—without compensation, meaningful attribution, or even notification—fundamentally changes the contributor's calculus. Why spend an hour crafting a detailed answer when the primary beneficiary is a proprietary AI company that will use that knowledge to build tools that may eventually make human Q&A obsolete?

This isn't hypothetical. The immediate reaction to the partnership announcement includes high-reputation users explicitly stating they will stop contributing. For a platform dependent on volunteer labor, this is an existential threat. Stack Overflow cannot pay contributors at scale (the economic model doesn't support it) and has now lost the moral high ground that motivated volunteer contributions.

The irony is acute: Stack Overflow needs fresh human contributions to keep OverflowAI current and accurate, but the partnership may be driving away the exact contributors who generate that value.

### The Rise of Alternative Platforms

**Where do developers go when they no longer trust Stack Overflow?** Several alternatives are gaining attention:

- **GitHub Discussions** — Already integrated into developer workflows, with clearer ownership by Microsoft that at least doesn't pretend to be a neutral community.
- **Discord communities and specialized forums** — Smaller, more controlled environments where content isn't being harvested for AI training at scale.
- **Self-hosted solutions and federated platforms** — The fediverse approach (Mastodon, Lemmy) offers potential for community-owned knowledge bases without centralized commercial exploitation.
- **Documentation-first approaches** — Projects increasingly investing in comprehensive docs rather than relying on Q&A platforms for knowledge dissemination.

The fragmentation risk is real. Stack Overflow succeeded by concentrating programming knowledge in one searchable, well-indexed location. If developers retreat to Discord servers, Slack communities, and private forums, that knowledge becomes harder to discover and less durable—potentially a net loss for the profession, even if individual contributors feel better about where their expertise lands.

## The AI Company's Perspective: Why OpenAI Wants This Data

**From OpenAI's perspective, the Stack Overflow deal is a strategic necessity.** Programming assistance is one of the most commercially valuable use cases for large language models, and training data quality directly determines model performance. Stack Overflow represents the largest, most vetted, most structured repository of practical programming knowledge on the internet—exactly what a coding model needs.

The value proposition for OpenAI is threefold:

1. **Verified technical knowledge** — Unlike random code scraped from GitHub or documentation, Stack Overflow content has been peer-reviewed. Answers are voted on, accepted by question-askers, and often include explanations of why solutions work. This curation is expensive to replicate.

2. **Coverage across the long tail** — Stack Overflow contains answers to obscure programming questions that appear rarely in training data but frequently in real developer workflows. A model trained without this data will struggle with edge cases, legacy systems, and niche frameworks.

3. **Natural language + code pairs** — Each Q&A represents a natural language question paired with a code answer—perfect training data for instruction-following models. The format is inherently aligned with how developers actually use AI assistants.

**The deal also serves a defensive purpose.** By securing formal access, OpenAI reduces legal risk (compared to continued scraping) and gains a marketing-friendly "partnership" narrative. They can claim attribution integration while competitors who scrape without deals look like bad actors. It's not just about the data—it's about positioning OpenAI as a responsible AI partner rather than a content vampire.

## What Happens Next: Scenarios and Predictions

**Three days into the partnership announcement, the trajectory is becoming clear.** The immediate crisis will likely stabilize, but the long-term implications for Stack Overflow, the developer community, and platform-community relationships will unfold over months and years.

**Short-term (May–June 2024):** The ban wave continues as Stack Overflow moderators crack down on content deletion protests. Some high-reputation users will leave permanently, either deleting accounts or simply ceasing contributions. The integration launches as planned—Stack Overflow has too much invested in the OpenAI deal to reverse course based on community backlash. Attribution features in ChatGPT arrive, satisfying legal requirements but likely disappointing contributors who expected individual credit.

**Medium-term (Late 2024):** The "chilling effect" on contributions becomes measurable. Stack Overflow sees declining new question and answer volumes, particularly from its most experienced contributors. The platform responds with AI-generated content filling gaps—exactly what the 2022 ban was designed to prevent—creating a quality degradation spiral. Enterprise customers for Stack Overflow Internal remain stable (the B2B product isn't affected by public platform drama), but advertising revenue on the public site declines as traffic drops.

**Long-term (2025+):** Two scenarios emerge:

| Scenario | Trigger | Outcome |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Scenario A: Stack Overflow survives** | AI attribution proves meaningful, contributors adapt to new reality, quality remains acceptable | Platform continues as diminished but functional resource; AI partnerships become normalized |
| **Scenario B: Community collapse** | Moderator exodus accelerates, content quality degrades, alternatives gain traction | Stack Overflow becomes historical archive rather than living resource; knowledge fragments across platforms |

The most likely outcome is somewhere between: Stack Overflow persists as a resource but never regains the trust of its most dedicated contributors. The platform transitions fully to a data licensing business, accepting that its community-driven phase is ending.

## The Fundamental Question: Who Owns Collective Knowledge?

**Beneath the specific grievances about attribution, compensation, and consent lies a deeper question: who owns knowledge when it is collectively created?** Stack Overflow's 59 million questions and answers represent perhaps the largest collaborative intellectual project in human history—hundreds of millions of hours of expert labor, contributed with the implicit understanding that it would benefit the programming profession broadly.

The AI era is forcing a reckoning with this question. When knowledge can be extracted, compressed, and regurgitated by models that never participated in its creation, the traditional boundaries of authorship and ownership blur. Is training an AI on Stack Overflow answers fundamentally different from a human developer reading those same answers, internalizing the patterns, and applying them in their work? If so, where is the line?

The contributor perspective is that human learning involves transformation, synthesis, and often attribution—a junior developer who learned from your Stack Overflow answer might cite it, thank you, or at least recognize the source of their knowledge. AI training extracts the knowledge without the social context, the reciprocity, or the recognition.

The platform perspective is that content posted publicly under permissive licenses is fair game for any use, including commercial AI training. The ToS say so. The licenses permit it. The legal framework supports it.

**The philosophical gap cannot be bridged by legal arguments.** It is a fundamental disagreement about what it means to contribute to a community knowledge base versus what it means to create content that becomes training data. Stack Overflow Inc. has chosen its answer: content is a commodity to be sold. The community's answer—content is a shared resource created in trust—is now in conflict with that choice.

This question extends far beyond Stack Overflow. Wikipedia, Reddit, Quora, Medium, and every other platform built on user-generated content faces the same reckoning. The Stack Overflow revolt is an early indicator of how communities will respond when they discover that their contributions have become the raw material for AI systems they never agreed to build.

## FAQ

### Q: Why are Stack Overflow users so angry about the OpenAI partnership?

**A:** Developers see the partnership as a betrayal of trust. Contributors spent years answering questions to help other developers, not to supply training data for proprietary AI models. **The core grievance is that volunteer labor is being monetized by Stack Overflow Inc. without consent, compensation, or meaningful attribution.** The fact that Stack Overflow previously banned ChatGPT-generated answers makes the partnership appear hypocritical.

### Q: Did Stack Overflow users agree to have their content used for AI training?

**A:** Not explicitly. While Stack Overflow's Terms of Service grant broad usage rights to the platform, **users contributed their content expecting it would remain a public resource for developers, not training fuel for commercial AI systems.** The ToS language predates the current AI training use case, and there's an ethical gap between what is legally permitted and what contributors reasonably understood they were agreeing to.

### Q: Can Stack Overflow legally license user-generated content to OpenAI?

**A:** Probably, but the legal situation is untested. **Stack Overflow's ToS grants the platform extensive rights to user content, and Creative Commons licenses (CC BY-SA) permit reuse with attribution.** However, whether AI model training constitutes a "derivative work" under copyright law—and thus whether CC share-alike requirements apply to trained models—remains an open question that courts have not yet resolved.

### Q: Are moderators actually deleting their content in protest?

**A:** Yes. **High-reputation users and moderators are deleting or defacing their answers to prevent them from being used as AI training data.** Stack Overflow has responded by banning users en masse for content deletion, treating the protests as vandalism rather than legitimate responses to changed platform terms.

### Q: How is this different from AI models scraping Stack Overflow already?

**A:** Scraping is informal and legally ambiguous; this partnership is formal and normalized. **OpenAI will now receive structured API access to 15 years of curated data, with attribution commitments and ongoing commercial terms.** The deal legitimizes and expands AI training access while giving Stack Overflow revenue it previously wasn't capturing from informal scraping.

### Q: Will this partnership make ChatGPT better at coding?

**A:** Almost certainly. **Stack Overflow contains the largest collection of vetted programming Q&A on the internet—59 million questions and answers covering the long tail of programming problems.** Access to this structured, peer-reviewed knowledge should improve ChatGPT's performance on edge cases, legacy systems, and niche frameworks that appear rarely in other training data.

### Q: What alternative platforms exist for developers leaving Stack Overflow?

**A:** Several options are gaining traction: **GitHub Discussions** (integrated into developer workflows), **Discord communities** (more controlled, less scrapable), **federated platforms like Lemmy** (community-owned, no centralized commercial exploitation), and **project-specific documentation** (maintained by individual projects rather than centralized Q&A platforms). Each has trade-offs in discoverability versus control.

### Q: Has Stack Overflow leadership responded to the backlash?

**A:** CEO Prashanth Chandrasekar has defended the partnership as a necessary business evolution. **He acknowledges that data licensing is now a core business model and describes ChatGPT as an "existential moment" that required the company to adapt.** He has hinted at potential future revenue sharing with contributors but offered no concrete commitments or timelines.

### Q: Does this partnership violate Creative Commons licenses?

**A:** Unclear, and likely to remain so until courts rule. **CC BY-SA requires attribution and that derivative works be shared under the same license.** If AI model training creates a "derivative work," OpenAI might theoretically be required to release model weights under open licenses. AI companies generally argue training is fair use, not derivative work creation—a position that has not been definitively tested in court for generative AI.

### Q: What precedent does this set for other community platforms?

**A:** It normalizes the "platform playbook": **build network effects on volunteer labor, then monetize through exclusive AI training data deals without revenue sharing.** Reddit has already followed the same template with its OpenAI partnership. Wikipedia, Quora, and other UGC platforms face similar pressures. The precedent suggests community platforms will increasingly treat contributor content as proprietary assets rather than shared resources.

## Conclusion: A Watershed Moment for Platform-Community Relations

**The Stack Overflow OpenAI partnership represents a hinge point in the relationship between community platforms and their contributors.** Whether this proves to be a sustainable business model or the beginning of a community collapse will depend on whether platforms can find a balance between monetization and the trust that makes volunteer contribution possible.

The lesson for platform builders is clear: **the knowledge your community creates is valuable, but the trust that motivates them to create it is priceless—and far easier to destroy than to rebuild.** Treating contributions as proprietary data assets may generate short-term revenue but risks the long-term sustainability of the platform itself.

For developers and contributors, the episode signals a new era of vigilance about where expertise is shared and how it may be used. **The default assumption that public platforms serve the community may need to give way to more intentional choices about which platforms deserve contributions—and under what terms.**

For the AI industry, the backlash is a reminder that training data is not merely a technical input but a social relationship. The quality of Stack Overflow's knowledge came from a community that felt ownership of their contributions. If that community fragments, the knowledge base that makes AI coding assistants useful degrades with it.

The next few months will reveal whether Stack Overflow can weather this storm or whether it has fundamentally miscalculated what makes its platform valuable. The knowledge is in the database, yes—but the curation, the quality control, the ongoing expertise that keeps it current comes from people. And people can leave.
